1. What is VGA? (Background you don't have yet)

VGA (Video Graphics Array) is an analog video display standard introduced by IBM in 1987 — the same era as the PS/2 keyboard from the last lab. It is the blue, D-shaped 15-pin plug you have seen on the back of older monitors and projectors.

The crucial idea is that VGA was designed to drive a CRT (cathode-ray tube) monitor — an old, heavy glass screen in which an electron beam is physically swept across the glass to light up phosphor dots. Even though you will be driving a modern flat panel, the monitor still pretends to be a CRT and expects exactly the same timing. So to talk to it, you must reproduce the rhythm of an electron beam sweeping the screen.

VGA is a great teaching protocol because:

  • There is no handshake and no acknowledge — the FPGA just keeps shouting pixels and sync pulses, forever, on a fixed schedule. It is a pure "open-loop" timing exercise.
  • The color is just three analog voltages (Red, Green, Blue) plus two digital sync signals (Hsync, Vsync). Five wires carry everything.
  • Everything is driven by counting cycles of a single pixel clock. If you can build two counters, you can build VGA.
In one sentence: VGA is an analog video link where your FPGA continuously paints the screen one pixel at a time, left-to-right and top-to-bottom, and inserts precisely-timed "sync" pulses to tell the monitor when to jump back to the start of the next line and the next frame.
The big contrast with PS/2: PS/2 was an input protocol where the device supplied the clock and you reacted to its falling edges. VGA is an output protocol where you are the master of time — you generate the pixel clock and every signal is just a function of where you are in your counters.

2. The Physical Connector & Pins

The VGA connector is a 15-pin D-SUB (DE-15), arranged in three rows of five. Of the 15 pins, only five really matter for this lab: the three color channels and the two sync signals.

Red (pin 1) Green (pin 2) Blue (pin 3) Hsync (pin 13) Vsync (pin 14)
PinNamePurpose
1REDAnalog red intensity (0–0.7 V)
2GREENAnalog green intensity (0–0.7 V)
3BLUEAnalog blue intensity (0–0.7 V)
13HSYNCHorizontal sync — "end of line, go back to the left"
14VSYNCVertical sync — "end of frame, go back to the top"
5,6,7,8,10GNDGrounds / color returns
4,9,11,12,15—Not needed here (ID / DDC / unused)

Where do the analog voltages come from?

The FPGA only outputs digital 1s and 0s, so how do you make an analog 0–0.7 V color? The Basys3 board has a tiny resistor-ladder DAC wired to the FPGA pins. Each color channel is driven by 4 FPGA pins through weighted resistors, so 4 bits become 16 analog voltage levels per channel. That is why vgaRed, vgaGreen, and vgaBlue are each 4 bits wide in your design.

4 bits × 3 channels = 12 bits of color = the pixel_color[11:0] signal in your top-level diagram. That gives 212 = 4096 possible colors.

3. How a Screen is Drawn: Raster Scanning

The screen is painted exactly the way you read a page of English text: left-to-right, then top-to-bottom. This zig-zag path is called a raster scan. There is no concept of "draw a rectangle here" — the monitor only understands "here is the color of the next pixel, now the next, now the next…".

The beam starts at the top-left pixel and sweeps right, painting one row of pixels.
When it reaches the right edge, the beam is switched off and flies back to the left — this is the horizontal retrace. The Hsync pulse happens during this trip.
It drops down one line and paints the next row. Repeat for all 600 visible rows.
After the last row, the beam flies all the way back to the top-left — the vertical retrace. The Vsync pulse happens during this trip.
One full sweep of the whole screen is a frame. At 72 Hz this whole picture is redrawn 72 times every second.
Key mental model: your FPGA is not "putting an image on the screen." It is a metronome that, on every single pixel-clock tick, answers the question "what color is the dot the beam is touching right now?" — and occasionally raises a sync flag to send the beam home.

4. Sync, Porches & the "Bigger Virtual Screen"

These are the three things everyone trips on, and they are really one idea seen from three angles: what is a sync pulse, why do my counters run past 800×600, and why are the black slices called "porches"? Let's take them in that order.

4.1 — What a "sync" pulse actually is

The thing nobody tells you up front: the monitor is blind. The only wires it receives are three analog color voltages and two digital pulses. There is no pixel address on the wire, no "this is row 3, column 57," no clock it can read. So how does it know where on the glass to put each color it's handed?

It uses a stopwatch. The monitor and your FPGA agree on exactly one rule: "the instant a sync pulse appears is time zero. Start drawing from a fixed corner and just measure time from that edge." Every pixel's position is inferred purely from how long it has been since the last sync pulse. Sync is the shared starting gun — nothing more, nothing less.

  • Hsync (horizontal sync) = "this line is finished — snap back to the left edge and begin the next row."
  • Vsync (vertical sync) = "this whole picture is finished — snap back to the top-left corner and begin a fresh frame."

Because position is measured as time since the last sync edge, those edges must land at exactly the right instant, every line and every frame, forever. If your sync timing drifts even slightly, the monitor's stopwatch points at the wrong spot and the picture rolls, tears, or reports "out of range."

Analogy — an old typewriter. Typing characters across the page = the visible pixels. Slamming the carriage-return lever (ding!) = Hsync: throw the carriage back to the left and roll down one line. Pulling the sheet out and feeding a blank page = Vsync: back to the very top. The bell prints nothing — it is pure timing — and that is precisely what a sync pulse is.

4.2 — Why the counters run past 800×600 (the "bigger virtual screen")

You noticed your hcount/vcount climb to 1040 × 666 even though the screen is only 800 × 600. You're not imagining it: the design genuinely scans a rectangle larger than anything you can see. Here's why.

Back to the CRT. When the beam finishes a visible line at the right edge, it can't teleport home — the magnets steering it need physical time to swing back to the left. During that swing the beam must be forced black (blanked), or it would smear a bright diagonal streak across the picture. Same at the bottom: after the last visible line the beam needs time to fly from bottom-right all the way back up to top-left.

So the standard pads every line and every frame with extra black, invisible time:

  • Horizontally: 800 visible + 240 invisible = 1040 clocks per line.
  • Vertically: 600 visible + 66 invisible = 666 lines per frame.

Picture your real image as a small box sitting inside a larger timing canvas. The beam visits every cell of the big canvas on a fixed schedule, but only paints inside the small inner box — everywhere else it is held black:

Those extra rows and columns are never displayed. They are pure housekeeping time during which the sync pulse fires and the beam repositions. Your counters keep ticking straight through them — which is exactly why the counters are bigger than the resolution. The clock doesn't pause during the "commercial break"; it simply doesn't draw.

So "is there a bigger virtual display?" Yes — but in time, not in pixels. A larger timing rectangle (1040×666) is wrapped around the smaller visible rectangle (800×600). The gap between them is the blanking region, and it exists entirely to give the beam room to fly back and to carry the sync pulses.

4.3 — Front porch, sync, back porch

That invisible black time isn't one lump — on each axis it's split into three named slices arranged around the sync pulse:

  • Front porch — a short black gap after the visible pixels but before the sync pulse (lets the line settle to black before the "go home" command).
  • Sync pulse — the actual "snap back" command described in 4.1.
  • Back porch — a short black gap after the sync pulse, giving the beam time to arrive and settle before real pixels resume.

Order along one line: VISIBLE → front porch → sync → back porch → (repeat). The same three-part pattern repeats vertically in units of whole lines.

Where the silly name comes from: on an oscilloscope the analog video voltage shows two flat black "shelves" on either side of the sync pulse. To 1930s television engineers they looked like the flat front porch and back porch of a house, with the sync pulse as the step down between them — and the nickname simply stuck.

5. Horizontal Timing (one line)

This lab uses the 800×600 @ 72 Hz VGA mode with a 50 MHz pixel clock. One full horizontal line is 1040 pixel-clocks long, which matches the assignment's XCoord range of 0–1039.

RegionWidth (pixel-clocks)hcount range
Visible / active video8000 – 799
Front porch56800 – 855
Sync pulse120856 – 975
Back porch64976 – 1039
Total line10400 – 1039

Generating Hsync is then a single comparison on your horizontal counter: 

// 800x600@72 horizontal sync (positive polarity)
// pulse is active while hcount is inside the sync window
wire Hsync = (hcount >= 856 && hcount <= 975);
Polarity matters. The 800×600@72 standard uses positive sync (the pulse is a logic-high). If your monitor shows "no signal" or an off-center image even though counters look right, an inverted sync polarity is a prime suspect.

6. Vertical Timing (one frame)

The vertical axis works exactly the same way, but its unit is whole lines instead of pixels. The vcount counter advances by one every time hcount wraps around (i.e. once per line). A full frame is 666 lines, matching the assignment's YCoord range of 0–665.

// 800x600@72 vertical sync (positive polarity)
wire Vsync = (vcount >= 637 && vcount <= 642);
Sanity check the refresh rate: 50 MHz ÷ (1040 × 666) = 50 000 000 ÷ 692 640 ≈ 72.2 Hz. The assignment's warning that "72 Hz may not be supported by some monitors" is exactly this number.

7. The Regions Diagram (visible vs. blanking)

Combining horizontal and vertical timing splits every frame into a 2-D map. Only the green box is visible; everything outside it is blanking and the FPGA must output black ({R,G,B} = 0) there. 

wire visible = (hcount < 800) && (vcount < 600);

// the color the interface actually drives out:
assign {vgaRed, vgaGreen, vgaBlue} =
       visible ? pixel_color : 12'h000;   // black in blanking
The #1 beginner bug: driving real color during blanking. If you let any R/G/B value escape while the beam is in a porch or sync region, the monitor mis-locks and you get rolling, tearing, or "out of range." Force black outside the visible box — no exceptions.

8. Color Encoding: 12-bit RGB-444

The pixel_color[11:0] bus packs three 4-bit channels. The switches drive it directly: (sw[11:8], sw[7:4], sw[3:0]) = (R, G, B).

Remember the hardware: each nibble feeds a 4-bit resistor-ladder DAC on the Basys3, turning 0000…1111 into 16 analog voltage steps from 0 V (off) to ~0.7 V (full brightness) on that color pin.

9. How This Maps to Your FPGA Design

The top-level (Figure 1 in the assignment) has three modules: the timing engine, the painter, and the wrapper.

VGA_Interface

Inputs: clk, rstn, pixel_color[11:0]
Outputs: vgaRed[3:0], vgaGreen[3:0], vgaBlue[3:0], Hsync, Vsync, XCoord[10:0], YCoord[10:0]

The timing engine. Generates the 50 MHz pixel clock, runs hcount/vcount, derives sync + visible, gates the color, and reports the current coordinate back out.

Drawer

Inputs: clk, reset, sw[11:0], btnu, btnd, btnl, btnr, XCoord, YCoord
Output: pixel_color[11:0]

The painter. For the pixel currently being scanned, it decides: am I inside the rectangle? If yes → output sw color; if no → output background. Buttons grow the rectangle in each direction.

VGA_Top

The wrapper that wires Drawer.pixel_color → VGA_Interface.pixel_color and feeds VGA_Interface's XCoord/YCoord back into the Drawer. This is a feedback loop, but a harmless one: the interface says "I'm drawing pixel (x,y)" and the drawer instantly answers "then here's its color."

Skeleton for the counters & pixel clock

// --- 50 MHz pixel clock from 100 MHz system clock ---
reg pclk = 0;
always @(posedge clk) pclk <= ~pclk;     // divide by 2 -> 50 MHz

// --- horizontal & vertical counters ---
localparam H_TOTAL = 1040, V_TOTAL = 666;
reg [10:0] hcount = 0, vcount = 0;

always @(posedge pclk or negedge rstn) begin
    if (!rstn) begin
        hcount <= 0; vcount <= 0;
    end else begin
        if (hcount == H_TOTAL-1) begin
            hcount <= 0;
            vcount <= (vcount == V_TOTAL-1) ? 0 : vcount + 1;
        end else begin
            hcount <= hcount + 1;
        end
    end
end

// --- registered outputs (assignment note #7: use flip-flops!) ---
always @(posedge pclk) begin
    Hsync <= (hcount >= 856 && hcount <= 975);
    Vsync <= (vcount >= 637 && vcount <= 642);
    if (hcount < 800 && vcount < 600)
        {vgaRed, vgaGreen, vgaBlue} <= pixel_color;
    else
        {vgaRed, vgaGreen, vgaBlue} <= 12'h000;
end
Why register the outputs? Assignment note #7: defining {Hsync, Vsync, vgaRed, vgaGreen, vgaBlue} as flip-flops means they all change cleanly on the same clock edge, with no combinational glitches racing to the connector. Glitches on a VGA line show up as visible speckle.

The 8× downsample trick (assignment hint #8)

// 800x600 is a lot of pixels to reason about. Drop the low 3 bits
// to work on a coarse 100x75 grid -> the rectangle math stays simple.
wire [10:0] x_ds = XCoord >> 3;   // 0..99
wire [10:0] y_ds = YCoord >> 3;   // 0..74

10. Common Pitfalls (Lab 6 Hints!)

  1. Leaking color into the blanking region. Always force {R,G,B}=0 unless hcount<800 && vcount<600. This is the most common cause of a black or rolling screen.
  2. Wrong sync polarity. 800×600@72 expects positive Hsync/Vsync. Inverting them gives a shifted, torn, or "out of range" picture. (Lab note #3: simulation must match the timing diagram exactly.)
  3. Driving logic on the 50 MHz clock incorrectly. Generate the pixel clock by dividing the 100 MHz clock (note #1). Don't try to use it as a true clock domain without care — many designs instead keep one 100 MHz domain and use a clock-enable that pulses every other cycle.
  4. Forgetting to register the outputs. Note #7 is explicit: sync and color must be flip-flops, not raw combinational comparisons, or you get visible glitches.
  5. Bouncing buttons. Note #9: a mechanical press bounces for milliseconds, so a single press would grow the rectangle dozens of times. Run btnu/d/l/r through a debouncer + one-shot.
  6. Lighting LEDs from the switches. Note #2 forbids it — the switches are color data only.
  7. Off-by-one counters. Total counts are 1040 and 666, so the counters run 0…1039 and 0…665. Using 1039/665 as the wrap point (not 1040/666) is the classic mistake that drifts your refresh rate.
If you internalize one sentence from this whole document: VGA is just two free-running counters (1040 wide, 666 tall) clocked at 50 MHz — every output signal (Hsync, Vsync, and whether to send real color or black) is a simple range-comparison on those two counters.
