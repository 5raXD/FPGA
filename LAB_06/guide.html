1 · The Big Picture

We built a circuit that draws on a computer monitor through the VGA port of the Basys3 board. On screen there is a rectangle. Its size is controlled by the four push-buttons (up / down / left / right grow each edge), and its color is set by the 12 switches. Everything outside the rectangle is black.

The one sentence to memorize: A monitor has no memory — it just sweeps an electron-beam-style cursor left-to-right, top-to-bottom, continuously. Our job is to feed it, in perfect time, two things: (1) sync pulses that tell it when to jump back to the start of a line/frame, and (2) the RGB color of whatever pixel the beam is currently on. The whole design is just a position counter (where is the beam now?) plus logic that answers "what color goes here?".

Why this matters / what's new vs. previous labs

In earlier labs the only outputs were LEDs and the 7-segment display. Here we output a real graphical image. The hard part is strict real-time timing: if a sync pulse is one pixel late, the picture rolls or tears. That's why timing must match the VGA standard exactly and why the sync outputs are flip-flops (registered), not raw combinational logic.

2 · How VGA Works — The Protocol

VGA is an analog raster protocol. The image is painted as a grid of lines (a "raster"). A virtual cursor scans:

  • Across one row, left → right (this is the horizontal scan).
  • At the end of a row it jumps back to the left and drops down one line.
  • After the last row it jumps back to the top-left (this is the vertical retrace), and a new frame begins.

The 4 regions of every line (and every frame)

For both the horizontal and vertical directions, one full sweep is split into 4 parts:

RegionWhat happensColor output
Visible / ActiveThe real picture is drawn here.Real RGB
Front porchShort blank gap before the sync pulse.Black (0,0,0)
Sync pulseThe Hsync / Vsync signal fires. Tells the monitor "retrace now".Black (0,0,0)
Back porchShort blank gap after the sync, before the next visible area.Black (0,0,0)
The porches + sync together are called the blanking interval. They exist for historical CRT reasons (the electron beam needs time to fly back), and modern monitors still require them to lock on. Rule: outside the visible box, you must output black, otherwise you'd "paint" during retrace.

The regions diagram (whole frame)

What the monitor actually receives

The Basys3 VGA connector has just 14 wires that matter: 4 Red + 4 Green + 4 Blue (a resistor-ladder DAC turns them into analog voltage), plus Hsync and Vsync (digital). There is no pixel clock wire — the monitor recovers timing purely from the sync pulses and the known standard. That's exactly why our timing must match a real VGA mode precisely.

3 · The Timing Numbers — 800×600 @ 72 Hz

Know this cold. Our mode is 800×600 @ 72 Hz with a 50 MHz pixel clock. (The assignment even warns that 72 Hz may not be supported by some monitors.) Don't confuse it with 800×600@60 (40 MHz).

Where the constants in the code come from

localparam H_VISIBLE = 799, H_FRONT_PORCH = 855, H_SYNC_END = 975, H_TOTAL = 1039;
localparam V_VISIBLE = 599, V_FRONT_PORCH = 636, V_SYNC_END = 642, V_TOTAL = 665;
  • H_VISIBLE = 799 → last visible column (counts 0–799 = 800 pixels).
  • H_FRONT_PORCH = 855 → Hsync starts the count after this, i.e. at 856.
  • H_SYNC_END = 975 → Hsync ends here (high for 856–975 = 120 clocks).
  • H_TOTAL = 1039 → last count of the line; then wrap to 0.

The refresh-rate sanity check (great oral answer)

pixel clock      = 50 MHz
clocks per frame = 1040 × 666 = 692,640
frame rate       = 50,000,000 / 692,640 ≈ 72.2 Hz   ✔  (that's the "72 Hz")
line rate        = 50,000,000 / 1040    ≈ 48.1 kHz
Sync polarity: 800×600@72 uses positive sync for both H and V. In our code Hsync <= (h_count > H_FRONT_PORCH) && (h_count <= H_SYNC_END) makes Hsync =1 during the pulse → active-high → positive polarity. Matches the standard.

Data flow every pixel-clock tick

  1. VGA_Interface advances its counters and publishes the current beam position as XCoord (=hcount) and YCoord (=vcount).
  2. Drawer looks at X,Y, decides "is this pixel inside the rectangle?", and outputs a 12-bit pixel_color (the switches' color if inside, else black).
  3. VGA_Interface takes that color back, and on the visible region drives it to the RGB pins; in blanking it forces black; meanwhile it generates Hsync/Vsync.

VGA_Top — the glue (no logic of its own)

wire [10:0] XCoord, YCoord;
wire [11:0] pixel_color;

VGA_Interface vga_if(.clk(clk), .rstn(~reset), .pixel_color(pixel_color),
                     .vgaRed(vgaRed), .vgaGreen(vgaGreen), .vgaBlue(vgaBlue),
                     .Hsync(Hsync), .Vsync(Vsync),
                     .XCoord(XCoord), .YCoord(YCoord));

Drawer drawer(.clk(clk), .reset(reset), .sw(sw),
              .btnu(btnu), .btnd(btnd), .btnl(btnl), .btnr(btnr),
              .XCoord(XCoord), .YCoord(YCoord), .pixel_color(pixel_color));
Note .rstn(~reset): the button gives active-high reset, but the interface wants an active-low reset (rstn = "reset, n for active-low"), so we invert it. The Drawer uses the active-high reset directly.

5 · VGA_Interface — Line by Line

File: VGA_Interface.v

This is the timing engine. Three jobs: (a) make a 50 MHz tick from the 100 MHz clock, (b) run the H/V position counters, (c) generate sync pulses and drive RGB.

① The 50 MHz pixel-clock enable

reg pix_en = 0;
always @(posedge clk) pix_en <= ~pix_en;   // toggles every 100 MHz cycle
Important design choice: we do not create a second real clock. clk stays at 100 MHz everywhere. pix_en is high every other cycle, so it's a clock-enable that's true at a 50 MHz rate. All the timing logic runs only if (pix_en). This is the recommended FPGA style: a single clock domain + an enable, instead of gating/dividing the clock (which causes timing/skew problems and "non-clocked cell" warnings).

② The position counters

always @(posedge clk) begin
    if (!rstn) begin h_count <= 0; v_count <= 0; end
    else if (pix_en) begin
        h_count <= `INC(h_count, H_TOTAL+1);          // 0..1039, then wrap to 0
        if (h_count == H_TOTAL)                       // end of a line?
            v_count <= `INC(v_count, V_TOTAL+1);      // advance row, 0..665
    end
end

The macro `define INC(x,max) ((x==max-1)? 0 : x+1) is just "increment with wrap-around". h_count counts every visible+blank column 0→1039. Only when a line finishes (h_count==1039) does v_count step to the next row. When v_count wraps from 665→0, a whole new frame starts. Together they're a 2-D address generator sweeping the beam position.

③ Sync generation + RGB output

always @(posedge clk) begin
    if (!rstn) begin Hsync<=0; Vsync<=0; vgaRed<=0; vgaGreen<=0; vgaBlue<=0; end
    else if (pix_en) begin
        Hsync <= (h_count > H_FRONT_PORCH) && (h_count <= H_SYNC_END);  // high 856..975
        Vsync <= (v_count > V_FRONT_PORCH) && (v_count <= V_SYNC_END);  // high 637..642
        if ((h_count <= H_VISIBLE) && (v_count <= V_VISIBLE)) begin     // visible?
            vgaRed   <= pixel_color[11:8];
            vgaGreen <= pixel_color[7:4];
            vgaBlue  <= pixel_color[3:0];
        end else begin
            vgaRed <= 0; vgaGreen <= 0; vgaBlue <= 0;   // blanking → black
        end
    end
end
  • Sync is a simple range-compare on the counters → a clean pulse at the right place.
  • Visible test h_count≤799 && v_count≤599: inside → output the Drawer's color; outside → forced black (this enforces the "black during blanking" rule).
  • Outputs are reg → flip-flops. The assignment explicitly requires Hsync, Vsync and the RGB channels to be registered so no combinational glitches reach the monitor.

④ Exposing the position to the Drawer

assign XCoord = h_count;   // 0..1039
assign YCoord = v_count;   // 0..665
One-pixel pipeline: the Drawer computes color combinationally from XCoord/YCoord, and the interface registers it on the next pix_en. So the RGB shown is delayed exactly one pixel-clock relative to the counter — and Hsync/Vsync are delayed the same one tick, so everything stays aligned. (This is why the testbench compares against a one-tick-delayed "expected".)

6 · Drawer — Line by Line

File: Drawer.v

The Drawer answers one question per pixel: "what color is pixel (X,Y)?" It keeps the rectangle's four edges in registers, grows them on button presses, and tests whether the current pixel is inside.

① Downsampling to a coarse 100×75 grid

localparam integer GRID_W = 100, GRID_H = 75;   // 800/8 , 600/8
localparam integer CX = GRID_W/2; // 50
localparam integer CY = GRID_H/2; // 37
...
wire [7:0] x_ds = XCoord[10:3];   // drop low 3 bits = divide by 8
wire [7:0] y_ds = YCoord[10:3];   // 800×600 → 100×75 "fat pixels"
Why downsample? The assignment asks for it. Working on a 100×75 grid means each rectangle "cell" is an 8×8 block of real pixels. The edge counters then only need to span 0–99 / 0–74 instead of 0–799 / 0–599 — fewer bits, simpler arithmetic, and one button press moves the edge a visible 8 pixels instead of an invisible 1. XCoord[10:3] is literally "integer divide by 8" for free (just a bit-slice, no divider).

② The rectangle's four edges (state)

reg [7:0] top, bottom, left, right;   // edges in grid units, inclusive

always @(posedge clk) begin
    if (reset) begin
        top <= CY; bottom <= CY;     // 37,37  → single cell
        left <= CX; right <= CX;      // 50,50  → "a dot" at center
    end else begin
        if (btnu_pulse && top    > 0)          top    <= top    - 1; // grow up
        if (btnd_pulse && bottom < GRID_H-1) bottom <= bottom + 1; // grow down
        if (btnl_pulse && left   > 0)          left   <= left   - 1; // grow left
        if (btnr_pulse && right  < GRID_W-1) right  <= right  + 1; // grow right
    end
end
  • On reset: all four edges collapse to the center cell (50,37) → the rectangle is a single dot. This is exactly what the assignment asks for at start-up.
  • btnu moves the top edge upward (decreasing row index) → rectangle grows up. Similarly btnd/btnl/btnr push the other edges outward.
  • The >0 / <GRID-1 guards clamp at the screen border so edges can't run off-screen or wrap around.
  • Each press uses a debounced single pulse, so one physical press = one cell of growth.
Note this design only ever grows. The buttons push edges outward; there's no shrink. To make the rectangle small again you press reset, which snaps it back to the center dot. (Good thing to be honest about if asked "can you shrink it?".)

③ The "is this pixel inside?" test

wire in_rect = (x_ds >= left) && (x_ds <= right)
            && (y_ds >= top ) && (y_ds <= bottom);

assign pixel_color = in_rect ? sw : 12'h000;

A pure combinational compare: if the coarse coordinate lies within the four edges, output the switch color sw; otherwise black. sw[11:8]=R, sw[7:4]=G, sw[3:0]=B. This is a continuous assign (no register) — the interface does the registering, so the Drawer stays a simple combinational "color function".

🔬 Drawer Deep-Dive

The whole module on one page

The Drawer is really just two pieces: a tiny state machine (4 edge registers that update on button pulses) and a combinational color function (compare current pixel to the edges). Nothing else. Here is everything it contains and how it connects:

Read it left-to-right: buttons → debouncers → edge registers (the only memory). In parallel, X/Y → ÷8 → comparator (uses the edges) → in_rect → MUX picks sw or black → pixel_color. The top half is sequential (updates rarely, on a press); the bottom half is combinational (recomputed for every pixel, ~50 M times/sec).

Width reasoning to say out loud: edges need to reach 99 (GRID_W−1) → 7 bits would only reach 127, 8 bits is comfortable. x_ds is XCoord[10:3] = bits 10..3 = 8 bits, max value 1039/8 = 129, but in the visible region X≤799 so x_ds≤99 — it lines up with the grid exactly.

The two processes, separated

Sequential — the edge FSM

always @(posedge clk)
  if (reset) begin
    top<=CY; bottom<=CY;   // 37
    left<=CX; right<=CX;    // 50
  end else begin
    if(btnu_pulse && top>0)        top--;
    if(btnd_pulse && bottom<74)   bottom++;
    if(btnl_pulse && left>0)       left--;
    if(btnr_pulse && right<99)    right++;
  end

Runs every clock but only changes on a pulse. The four ifs are independent → you can grow two edges "at once" if two pulses coincide.

Combinational — the color

wire x_ds = XCoord[10:3];
wire y_ds = YCoord[10:3];

wire in_rect =
   (x_ds>=left)  & (x_ds<=right)
 & (y_ds>=top)   & (y_ds<=bottom);

assign pixel_color =
   in_rect ? sw : 12'h000;

No clock, no state. It's a pure function color = f(X, Y, edges, sw) evaluated for every single pixel.

Coordinate system & the "inclusive" bounds

Edges are stored as inclusive grid indices. A single cell means left==right and top==bottom — and because the test uses >=/<=, that one cell is still "inside", so you get a visible dot (not an empty rectangle). Below: the grid, the center dot at reset, and the same rectangle after a few presses.

Each cell on the coarse grid is 8×8 real pixels, so one press visibly moves an edge by 8 px. The rectangle area in real pixels = (right−left+1)·8 × (bottom−top+1)·8.

Timing subtlety: edges vs. pixels

The edge registers update on posedge clk (100 MHz), but a human press lasts millions of cycles, so for the entire duration the beam is scanning, the edges are stable. That means the rectangle is drawn consistently across the whole frame — no tearing — even though the color logic is purely combinational. The only time edges change is the single clock when a debounced pulse fires; that's far faster than one frame, so at most one frame could show a 1-cell transition (invisible to the eye).

Common "gotcha" questions about the Drawer

Why does reset give a dot and not nothing?
Because the bounds are inclusive (>=/<=) and reset sets left==right, top==bottom. So exactly one cell satisfies in_rect → a single 8×8 dot at the center, which is what the spec asks for at start-up.
Why store edges in grid units instead of pixel units?
Smaller registers (8-bit vs 11-bit), simpler compares, and one press = one visible 8-px step. We convert the incoming pixel coordinate down to grid units with a free bit-slice ([10:3]) and compare in the same units.
What if I press up and down at the same instant?
The four if statements are independent, so both top and bottom update on that clock — the rectangle grows in both directions simultaneously. They never conflict because each button only touches its own edge register.
Is pixel_color a register or a wire? Why does it matter?
A wire (continuous assign) — combinational. The Drawer must produce the right color the same cycle the interface presents (X,Y); the interface then registers it. If the Drawer also registered, we'd add an extra pixel of delay and would have to account for it. Keeping it combinational keeps the pipeline a clean one-stage delay.

7 · Debouncer — How & Why

File: Debouncer.v (reused from Lab 1/4)

The problem: a mechanical button doesn't switch cleanly — its contact "bounces", producing a burst of fast 0/1 flickers for a few milliseconds. At 100 MHz that single press could register as hundreds of presses. We'd get the rectangle jumping wildly.

The idea: a hysteresis up/down counter

always @(posedge clk) begin
    if (input_unstable == 1)
        counter <= (counter < all_ones) ? counter+1 : counter;  // count up while pressed
    else
        counter <= (counter > 0) ? counter-1 : counter;       // count down while released

    prev_msb      <= counter[MSB];
    output_stable <= (~prev_msb) & counter[MSB];   // 1-cycle pulse on 0→1 of MSB
end
  • While the button reads 1, the counter climbs; while it reads 0, it falls. Brief bounces only nudge it a little — it takes a sustained press to fill it. That's the hysteresis (noise immunity).
  • We watch the counter's MSB. When the MSB flips 0→1, the press is "confirmed".
  • output_stable = (~prev_msb) & counter[MSB] is a rising-edge detector → it emits exactly one clock pulse per confirmed press, no matter how long you hold. That single pulse is what moves the rectangle edge by one cell.

We instantiate it with COUNTER_BITS = 20. The MSB flips after ~219 ≈ 524,288 cycles of "mostly pressed", i.e. about 5 ms at 100 MHz — long enough to swallow bounce, short enough to feel instant.

Debouncer #(.COUNTER_BITS(20)) db_u (.clk(clk), .input_unstable(btnu), .output_stable(btnu_pulse));
// ...same for btnd, btnl, btnr → four independent debouncers
Why "single pulse" matters here: if the Drawer saw a level (held = 1) instead of a pulse, holding the button would grow the rectangle by one cell every clock = instantly off-screen. The pulse guarantees one-press-one-step. The parameter COUNTER_BITS being a parameter (not hard-coded) also satisfies the lab's "modules must be parametric" rule.

8 · Color Encoding (12-bit)

Basys3 gives 4 bits per channel → 12-bit color = 4096 colors. The switches map directly:

BitsChannelSwitches
pixel_color[11:8] Redsw[11:8]
pixel_color[7:4] Greensw[7:4]
pixel_color[3:0] Bluesw[3:0]

Examples: 12'hF00 = pure red, 12'h0F0 = green, 12'hFFF = white, 12'h000 = black. The 4 bits drive a resistor-ladder DAC on the board that produces the 0–0.7 V analog level the monitor expects on each color pin.

10 · The Testbench

File: VGA_Interface_tb.v

It drives a 100 MHz clock, holds reset for 20 ns, then runs one full frame and self-checks the interface against an independent model.

  • Clock: always #5 clk = ~clk; → 10 ns period = 100 MHz.
  • Stimulus: on each pix_en it feeds a $random color while in the visible region (else 0), mimicking what the Drawer would send.
  • Self-check (the clever part): it keeps a one-tick-delayed copy of the expected color and coordinates (x_d, y_d, expected_pixel_color) because the DUT registers its outputs one pix_en later. Then it asserts:
    • RGB == expected color in visible region, else == black;
    • Hsync high exactly when x_d is in the sync window;
    • Vsync high exactly when y_d is in the sync window.
  • correct accumulates with & over the whole frame; at the end it prints "Test passed!!" only if every pixel matched.
  • Frame length: (H_TOTAL+1)*(V_TOTAL+1)*2 — the ×2 is because two 100 MHz cycles = one pixel-clock tick.
Oral-ready takeaway: the bench proves the interface's timing is bit-exact — sync edges land on the right counts and color is blanked outside the visible box — which is the whole correctness criterion for a VGA controller.

11 · Problems We Hit & How We Fixed Them

ProblemFix
Need a 50 MHz pixel clock from a 100 MHz board clock without gating the clock. Used a clock-enable (pix_en toggling every cycle) inside one 100 MHz domain instead of a divided clock.
Buttons bouncing → rectangle jumping many cells per press. 20-bit debouncer producing a single 1-cycle pulse per press.
Combinational glitches on sync/RGB could distort the picture. Made Hsync, Vsync and all RGB channels registered flip-flops.
Vivado TIMING-17 "non-clocked sequential cell" warnings if the /2 clock is used. Avoided the generated clock entirely (enable approach); documented the alternative in the XDC.
Edges could run off-screen. Added border clamps (top>0, right<GRID_W-1, …).
72 Hz mode not supported by some monitors (assignment warning). Verified on a monitor that supports 800×600@72; timing matches the standard exactly.

12 · Likely Oral-Exam Questions ⭐

Click each to reveal a crisp answer. These are the ones examiners love.

Explain your design in one minute.
A VGA controller on the Basys3. VGA_Interface generates the 800×600@72 timing: it makes a 50 MHz pixel-clock enable from the 100 MHz clock, runs horizontal/vertical counters that track the beam position, generates Hsync/Vsync at the right counts, and drives RGB only in the visible region (black elsewhere). It hands the current (X,Y) to the Drawer, which keeps a rectangle's four edges on a coarse 100×75 grid, grows them with debounced buttons, colors inside-pixels with the switch value and outside-pixels black, and returns that color to the interface. VGA_Top just wires the two together.
What are the front porch, sync, and back porch — and why do they exist?
They're the blanking parts of each scan. Front porch = blank gap before the sync pulse; sync pulse = the Hsync/Vsync signal that tells the monitor to retrace; back porch = blank gap after sync before the visible area. Historically a CRT needs time for the electron beam to fly back to the start of the next line/frame; modern monitors still use these intervals to lock onto the signal. During all of them we output black.
Why 50 MHz? How did you get it from 100 MHz?
50 MHz is the pixel clock for 800×600@72 (1040×666 clocks/frame × 72 Hz ≈ 50 MHz). I didn't divide the clock; I made a clock-enable pix_en that toggles every 100 MHz cycle, so it's true every other cycle = a 50 MHz rate. All timing logic runs if (pix_en). Single clock domain, no clock gating — the clean FPGA way.
Why is the refresh rate 72 Hz? Show the math.
50,000,000 pixel-clocks/s ÷ (1040 × 666 clocks/frame) = 50e6 / 692,640 ≈ 72.2 Hz. The horizontal line rate is 50e6/1040 ≈ 48.1 kHz.
Why must Hsync, Vsync and RGB be registered (flip-flops)?
To remove combinational glitches. Combinational logic settles through hazards; if those reached the monitor the picture could tear or show specks. Registering the outputs makes them change cleanly once per pixel-clock, perfectly aligned. The assignment explicitly requires it.
Why downsample by 8 to 100×75?
Simplicity and usability. Each grid cell becomes an 8×8 block, so the edge counters need only 0–99 / 0–74 (8-bit) instead of full 0–799 / 0–599, and a single button press moves an edge a visible 8 pixels. XCoord[10:3] is a free divide-by-8 (just a bit-slice — no divider hardware).
How does the debouncer work, and why a single pulse?
A hysteresis up/down counter: counts up while the button reads 1, down while 0. Brief bounces barely move it; only a sustained press fills it and flips the MSB. A rising-edge detector on the MSB (~prev_msb & msb) emits exactly one 1-cycle pulse per press. Single pulse = one press grows the rectangle by one cell; a level would grow it every clock and shoot off-screen.
What does the Drawer output for a pixel outside the rectangle?
Black, 12'h000. pixel_color = in_rect ? sw : 12'h000. And even if the Drawer output something nonzero during blanking, the interface forces black outside the visible region anyway.
What happens on reset?
Board reset is active-high; the interface gets ~reset (active-low rstn). On reset the counters and outputs clear, and the Drawer collapses all four edges to the center cell (50,37) → the rectangle becomes a single dot at screen center, as required at start-up.
Can you shrink the rectangle?
Not with the buttons — they only push edges outward (grow). To make it small again you press reset, which snaps it back to the center dot. (Honest limitation; could be extended with a "shrink" mode.)
How is color encoded? How many colors?
12-bit: 4 bits each for R, G, B → 4096 colors. sw[11:8]=R, sw[7:4]=G, sw[3:0]=B. The 4 bits per channel feed a resistor-ladder DAC on the board producing the analog 0–0.7 V the VGA monitor expects.
Why is there no pixel-clock wire to the monitor?
VGA is analog and self-timed: the monitor recovers timing from the Hsync/Vsync pulses plus the known standard mode. That's precisely why our counts and porches must match a real VGA mode exactly — there's no clock to "tell" the monitor otherwise.
What's the sync polarity, and where is it set in code?
Positive (active-high) for both H and V in 800×600@72. In code Hsync <= (h_count > H_FRONT_PORCH) && (h_count <= H_SYNC_END) drives Hsync to 1 during the pulse window → active-high.
Why does the testbench delay the expected values by one tick?
Because the interface registers its outputs: the RGB for a given (X,Y) appears one pix_en later. So the bench compares the DUT against a one-tick-delayed model (x_d, y_d, expected_pixel_color) to stay aligned. It also delays the sync checks the same way.
What limits the maximum rectangle size?
The border clamps: top/left stop at 0, bottom at GRID_H−1 (74), right at GRID_W−1 (99). Fully grown it fills the whole 100×75 grid = entire 800×600 screen.
