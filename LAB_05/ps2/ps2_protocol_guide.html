1. What is PS/2? (Background you don't have yet)

PS/2 is an old, simple, serial communication protocol invented by IBM in 1987 for connecting keyboards and mice to a PC. The connector is the small round purple/green plug your dad used to plug into the back of a computer before USB became universal.

Even though USB has replaced it on most modern computers, PS/2 is perfect for teaching because:

  • It uses only 2 wires for communication (plus power + ground).
  • It is slow (10–16.7 kHz), so an FPGA running at 100 MHz has tons of time to react.
  • It is synchronous serial — the keyboard supplies its own clock, so you don't need any complicated clock recovery.
  • The frame format is fixed and tiny (11 bits per byte), making decoding easy.
In one sentence: PS/2 is a 2-wire serial link where the keyboard sends you one byte at a time, wrapped in a tiny 11-bit envelope, and it provides its own clock pulses to tell you exactly when each bit is valid.

2. The Physical Connector & Pins

The PS/2 connector is a 6-pin mini-DIN. Of the 6 pins, only 4 actually matter:

Pin #NameColor in diagramPurpose
1DATAgreenCarries one bit at a time (the actual message)
2—grayNot connected
3GNDblackGround (0 V reference)
4VCCred+5 V power for the keyboard
5CLKyellowClock signal generated by the keyboard
6—grayNot connected
The whole game from an FPGA perspective is just two signals: PS2Clk and PS2Data. Power and ground are handled by the Basys3 board for you.

Electrical behavior — "Open-collector"

Both CLK and DATA are open-collector with pull-up resistors. Practically this means:

  • The line floats high (logic 1) when nobody is talking.
  • Either side (keyboard or host) can pull it low (logic 0).
  • This allows bi-directional communication on the same wires.

3. The Two Signals: Clock & Data

The keyboard only generates clock pulses when it has something to say. The rest of the time, both lines just sit idle at logic 1. This is one of the most important quirks of PS/2 — and the source of Important Design Note #3 in your assignment.

CLK (yellow) DATA (green)
Very important: Do not design your FPGA module as if PS2Clk were a continuous oscillator. It only ticks during a transmission, then stops. Your logic must therefore react to the falling edges of PS2Clk while they exist, and not wait for a clock that may never come.

When do you sample DATA?

The keyboard guarantees that DATA is stable on the falling edge of CLK. So your rule is simple: 

// Pseudo-code
on every falling_edge(PS2Clk):
    capture PS2Data into the next position of a shift register

4. The 11-Bit Transmission Frame

Every single byte the keyboard sends is wrapped in an envelope of 11 bits:

BitNameValueMeaning
1Start0Tells host "a frame is starting"
2–9Data 0..70/1The 8-bit payload (scan code), LSB first
10Parity0/1Odd parity: total number of 1s in bits 2–10 is odd
11Stop1Tells host "frame complete, line idle"
Key takeaway: the actual data byte you care about (the scan code) is just 8 of the 11 bits. The other 3 are an envelope — but you must still shift them in, otherwise your timing gets misaligned.

5. Reading FROM the Keyboard (Device → Host)

This is the only direction you need for Lab 5. Here is the full picture, bit by bit.

The full waveform

Step-by-step algorithm

Idle. Both CLK and DATA are high. Your FPGA waits, watching for a falling edge on CLK.
CLK falls. DATA is low — that's the Start bit. Shift it into bit 0 of an 11-bit shift register.
On each of the next 8 falling edges, sample DATA and shift it in. You are collecting D0…D7, LSB first.
On the 10th falling edge, capture the parity bit. (You may verify it or just trust the keyboard.)
On the 11th falling edge, capture the stop bit. It should be 1. If it isn't, the frame was corrupt.
Now bits 1..8 of your shift register hold the scan code byte. Latch it into scancode[7:0].
Lab 5 design hint #3 says: "Try analyzing the packet before the last cycle." Why? Because once the stop bit arrives, the clock stops toggling — if your FSM was waiting for an 11th falling edge that has already happened to then output the result, you'll be stuck. Output the byte on the 10th (parity) edge instead, while the clock is still alive.

6. Writing TO the Keyboard (Host → Device)

You don't need this for Lab 5, but it's worth a glance to round out your understanding. To send a command (e.g. "turn on Caps-Lock LED"), the host must briefly take control of both lines:

  1. Pull CLK low for at least 100 µs (this tells the keyboard "stop talking, I want to send").
  2. Pull DATA low, then release CLK.
  3. The keyboard now generates the clock pulses, and the host puts each bit on DATA at the right time.
  4. Same 11-bit envelope is used, with one extra bit at the end (the device's ACK).
The keyboard always supplies the clock, even when it is the host that is talking. The host only asks for permission to transmit by pulling CLK low.

7. Scan Codes: Make, Break, Extended

The 8-bit byte the keyboard sends you is not ASCII. It is a scan code — an arbitrary number assigned to each physical key. The same physical key produces different bytes depending on what just happened to it:

EventWhat the keyboard sendsExample: key 'A'
MAKE — you press the key The scan code itself 0x1C
REPEAT — you hold the key The same scan code, over and over, every ~100 ms 0x1C 0x1C 0x1C …
BREAK — you release the key 0xF0 followed by the scan code 0xF0 0x1C
EXTENDED — arrow keys, numpad /, etc. 0xE0 followed by the scan code (and 0xE0 0xF0 xx on release) Right Arrow: 0xE0 0x74

Visualizing a key press & release

Lab 5 design hint #4 says: "Make sure that despite the numerous retransmissions of the make-code, you do not generate multiple pulses." That is exactly what the picture above is warning you about — your keyPressed pulse must fire only on the first 0x1C, not on every subsequent autorepeat.

8. How This Maps to Your FPGA Design

Your top-level design from Figure 1 of the assignment has two custom modules:

Ps2_Interface

Inputs: PS2Clk, PS2Data, rstn
Outputs: scancode[7:0], keyPressed

This is the module that actually speaks the PS/2 protocol. It contains the shift register and the small FSM that watches CLK falling edges.

Ps2_Display

Inputs: clk, rstn, keyPressed, scancode[7:0]
Outputs: seg[6:0], an[3:0], dp, led

Pure 100 MHz logic. It just displays the latest scancode in hex on the two rightmost 7-segment digits and pulses an LED whenever keyPressed goes high.

Suggested skeleton for Ps2_Interface (the design hint #1)

// 22-bit shift register approach
// Why 22? Because 2 frames × 11 bits = 22, so you can keep a brief
// history of the LAST byte and the CURRENT byte and decide what to
// do based on prefixes (0xE0, 0xF0) before the clock dies.

reg [21:0] shift_reg;
reg [3:0]  bit_count;     // counts 0..10 within one frame

// Detect falling edge of PS2Clk in the 100 MHz domain
reg ps2clk_d1, ps2clk_d2;
always @(posedge clk) {ps2clk_d2, ps2clk_d1} <= {ps2clk_d1, PS2Clk};
wire ps2clk_fall = ps2clk_d2 & ~ps2clk_d1;

always @(posedge clk or negedge rstn) begin
    if (!rstn) begin
        shift_reg <= 22'h3FFFFF;
        bit_count <= 0;
    end else if (ps2clk_fall) begin
        shift_reg <= {PS2Data, shift_reg[21:1]};   // shift right, MSB-in
        bit_count <= (bit_count == 10) ? 0 : bit_count + 1;
    end
end
Why two flip-flops on PS2Clk? Because PS2Clk comes from an asynchronous source (the keyboard) but you are sampling it in your 100 MHz domain. The two-flop synchronizer prevents metastability — which is exactly the topic of the Metastability.pptx sitting in this lab folder. (Asynchronous reset, design hint topic #1, is the other big metastability-related concept.)

9. Common Pitfalls (Lab 5 Hints!)

  1. Treating PS2Clk as a normal always-running clock. It isn't. It only ticks during a transmission. Use it as a signal in the 100 MHz domain, not as a Verilog posedge clock for your whole module.
  2. Waiting until bit 11 (the stop) before decoding. By then the clock has stopped. Decode on bit 10 (parity) — that's still inside a live clock pulse. (This is design hint #3.)
  3. Generating multiple keyPressed pulses while a key is held. The keyboard will resend the make-code every ~100 ms. Use a one-shot or a "armed/disarmed" flag. (Design hint #4.)
  4. Forgetting the 0xE0 prefix for extended keys. The numeric-keypad Enter, the arrow keys, and a few others come as E0 xx. Ignore the E0 and display only xx (assignment 2.a).
  5. Confusing D and 0, or B and 8, on the 7-segment. The assignment explicitly suggests lighting the decimal point when you draw D or B, or using lowercase "d" and "b" (assignment 2.a, last paragraph).
  6. Missing the metastability concept. Any asynchronous signal entering the 100 MHz domain (PS2Clk, PS2Data, the active-low reset) must go through a 2-stage flip-flop synchronizer. This is also test material per the lab goals.
If you internalize one sentence from this whole document: The PS/2 keyboard hands you 8 useful bits inside an 11-bit frame, clocked by its own clock that only ticks during transmission — your job is to shift the frame in on every falling edge, then turn the resulting byte into a one-shot pulse and a hex display.
